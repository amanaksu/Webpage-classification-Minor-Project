DENNIS SCHWARTZ Movie Reviews

TWO YEARS AT SEA (director: Ben Rivers; cinematographer: Ben Rivers; editor:  Ben Rivers; cast: Jake Williams; Runtime: 92; MPAA Rating: NR; producer: Ben Rivers; Cinema Guild; 2011-UK)

"Fascinating study of a self-sufficient man living with nature somewhere in the wilds of the Aberdeenshire forest."

Reviewed by Dennis Schwartz

The debut feature film for the British short film director Ben Rivers, who is also the producer/editor/cinematographer of this fascinating study of a self-sufficient man living with nature somewhere in the wilds of the Aberdeenshire forest. It's a non-commercial documentary, a high-concept experimental film that is shown in museums and arthouses and not in the multiplexes. The quiet film is almost dialogue free, except for the subject mumbling once in a Scottish brogue about his chesty cough, and pushes no agenda and thankfully comes without talking heads. The 40-year-old Rivers treks to the isolated woods in the Scottish Highlands to film the late middle-aged Walt Whitman bearded recluse Jake Williams, living in isolation with his black pussy cat in a dilapidated abode. Rivers uses a hand-cranked 16mm Bolex camera to film Williams, someone he filmed before for his 2006 short “This Is My Land.” The context of the film has Rivers unobtrusively filming Williams doing his daily routines, such as preparing chow, chopping wood, sleeping on a homemade raft while fishing in a desolate loch, and listening to country tunes on his gramophone. It's not mentioned but I read that this is the dream life Williams always aspired and spent two years at sea with the Merchant Navy to financially realize his dream of surviving alone in the woods. Like in the Robert Flaherty documentaries that started this genre, Rivers was coaxed into several activities by the director to bring more excitement to the documentary.

Though not a film for all tastes for those idealistic nature lovers who will take to it, it will probably be a mesmerizing experience because of its unstated radical proclamations and unflinching voice against bourgeois consumerist living. 

REVIEWED ON 8/12/2013       GRADE: A-
