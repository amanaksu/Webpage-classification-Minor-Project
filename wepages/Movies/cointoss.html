COIN TOSS (director: Satya Kharkar; screenwriter: story by Mary Trimble & Satya Kharkar/Mary Trimble; cinematographers: Robert Patrick Stern/Satya Kharkar; editors:  Chris Parker/Satya Kharkar; music:  Nik Phoenika/Robert Patrick Stern; cast: Joe Mastrino (Tom Bennett), Shirin Caiola (Linda), Steve Parks (Turley & Morley), Satya Kharkar (Agent Polecat), David Goodloe (Deputy), Paul Tinsley (Mr. B), Brent Foster (Doug), Heather VanderWielen (Mia), Shalaka Kulkami (Meera), Ashley Zych (Evie), Maggie Malone (Barbara), Rahul Kharkar (car thief), Saleel Raut (Dileep); Runtime: 97; MPAA Rating: NR; producer: Satya Kharkar; Anmol Movies; 2012)

 
"Competently made indie."

Reviewed by Dennis Schwartz

A direct to video low-budget romantic comedy, shot on video, from Chicago residing newcomer Satya Kharkar. It has some good moments trying to say something significant about white collar financial crime mixed with some awkward moments tied up in a convoluted subplot about evil schemers. The story is by Mary Trimble & Satya Kharkar, with Ms. Trimble writing the overworked screenplay. Though appealing in spots, the message film (you can't buy happiness) had too many rough spots to completely win me over. The stilted unnatural dialogue was a turn-off, as was the emotionally limited acting. Also the derivative story never had a steady flow, as it kept getting sidetracked with subplots that left too many loose ends and clumsy sudden twists. But for those who favor breezy romantic sitcoms, with a dash of screwball comedy and plenty of charm, and are tired of the formulaic big-budget mainstream films, this competently made indie might find favor for such a viewer because of its inspirational story line. Also the production values were fine and the Chicago location visuals were pleasing. If this small pic gets to the right audience, it might find some modest success.

Hard luck nice guy Tom Bennett (Joe Mastrino), always trying to do the right thing, is given his late dad's lucky silver dollar coin by his ailing small town publisher mother (Maggie Malone). When mom dies Tom's inheritance is up in the air because mom foolishly got involved with his Chicago residing untrustworthy conman billionaire cousin Doug (Brent Foster), who placed her investment in an illegal pyramid scheme that's investigated by the Feds. Tom goes by car with his gold-digging gorgeous fiancée Linda (Shirin Caiola) to see Doug and straighten out the problem. When things seem hopeless in the Windy City, Tom uses the lucky coin to buy a winning $350 million Powerball ticket.

But just because Tom won the lottery doesn't mean his troubles are over, as various ticklish situations arise. The story unfolds fairy tale like with the gullible Tom in the process of learning many life lessons and meeting a variety of strange characters. Thereby we are introduced to the wise homeless character Meera (Shalaka Kulkami) and to a new romantic interest for Tom in Mia (Heather VanderWielen), a former neighbor of his family. She's the nice girl, adopted when her folks died by her kindly math genius uncle Turley (Steve Parks). He's the good twin, while the eye patch wearing brother Mortimer (both twins are played by Parks) is the evil twin who stole his brother's money and ideas. We  learn that during her childhood Mia had a crush on the inattentive Tom. Now, in changed circumstances, we observe how a sweet romance is rekindled between the two nice people and how the sympathetic Tom tries to get his life back on the right track by learning the real value of winning and losing.

It's a good-natured pic, that is gently told and should suit the viewer looking for a positive film that tries to give a reassuring voice to nice folks.

REVIEWED ON 7/16/2013       GRADE: B-
