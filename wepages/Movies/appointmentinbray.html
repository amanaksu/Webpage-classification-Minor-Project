APPOINTMENT IN BRAY (RENDEZ-VOUS A BRAY) (director/writer: Andre Delvaux; screenwriter: from a Julien Gracq short story "Le roi Cophetua"; cinematographer: Ghislain Cloquet; editor: Nicole Berckmans; music:  Frédéric Devreese; cast: Anna Karina (Elle), Mathieu Carrière (Julien Eschenbach), Roger Van Hool (Jacques Neuil), Bulle Ogier (Odile), Martine Sarcey (Wife), Pierre Vernier (Husband), Jean Bouise (Restaurant Chef), Pierre Lampe (Soldier), Boby Lapointe (L'Aubergiste); Runtime: 90; MPAA Rating: NR; producer: Mag Bodard; Cinematex; 1971-Belgium/France-in French with English subtitles)

 
"A great film."

Reviewed by Dennis Schwartz

Andre Delvaux ("Woman Between Wolf and Dog"/"The Abyss"/"The Man Who Had Cut His Hair Short") based his delicate enigmatic film on the surrealistic French writer Julian Gracq' novella, “Le Roi Cophétua.” The film is structured more as a rondo (A, B, A, C, A, D . . .) than a narrative, and is difficult to narrow down as to what category it fits.

Julien (German actor Mathieu Carrière) lives in Paris in 1917, where the pianist studies music and works for a newspaper writing musical reviews, a job he got because his composer friend Jacques Neuil (Roger Van Hool) quit the job three years ago to join a French air squadron for the First World War. Receiving a telegram from Jacques that he has a leave and wants him to stay in his family country house in Bray, near the battle-front, during his return home. It pleases Julien, a non-combatant civilian from neutral Luxembourg, to meet again his dear friend, and he eagerly goes there by train. Julien is let into the mansion by the mute housekeeper (Anna Karina) and while he waits for his friend's arrival, he muses over his prewar encounters with Jacques and his girlfriend musician, Odile (Bulle Ogier). The mysterious servant acts as hostess, serving him tea and food. When Jacques is a no show, the mystery woman takes Julien to bed. We never learn why she was there, her relationship with Jacques or why Jacques was a no show--though we know Julien fears his friend is dead. The next morning Julien goes to the train station but impulsively decides not to board the train. The pic mysteriously ends on that incomplete note, as we wonder what Julien is up to next.

The film is constructed as seen through the eyes of Julien Eschenbach, the narrator, and his many flashbacks connect us with his encounters with Jacques. In a Proustian way it brings about a nostalgia for a cherished past, as we are left puzzling over its dreamlike story and enchanted by its rich visual beauty. A great film. It might be Delvaux's best one.

REVIEWED ON 5/30/2013       GRADE: A

Dennis Schwartz: "Ozus' World Movie Reviews"
