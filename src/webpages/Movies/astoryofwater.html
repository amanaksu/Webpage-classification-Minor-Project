A STORY OF WATER (UNE HISTOIRE D'EAU) (director/writer: Jean-Luc Godard/Francois Truffaut; cinematographer: Michael Latouche; editor:  Jean-Luc Godard; cast: Caroline Dim (Girl), Jean-Claude Brialy (Man), Jean-Luc Godard (Narrator); Runtime: 18; MPAA Rating: NR; producer: Pierre Braunberger; Janus Films; 1961-France-in French with English subtitles)

"Zany short as if it were a Mack Sennett slapstick comedy done on speed."

Reviewed by Dennis Schwartz

Co-directors and co-writers, Jean-Luc Godard and Francois Truffaut, French New Wave directors, film this zany short as if it were a Mack Sennett slapstick comedy done on speed and with a playful narration by Godard and a wacky Daffy Duck dialogue bringing up anything on the minds of the featured  two characters. This was one of five notable shorts Godard made in collaboration with his Cahiers du Cinema colleagues in the late '50s and early '60s, before going on to make solo feature films. Though Truffaut wrote the original script, Godard re-worked it by adding his own flippant narration.

A feisty young lady (Caroline Dim) steps out of her country apartment in a village located just outside of Paris, in February, to take the bus to work in Paris and finds the area flooded and no transportation available. She dons boots and hitches a ride on the flooded road to Paris with a young man (Jean-Claude Brialy) driving a Ford, whom she describes as someone who "Wasn’t bad at all." They chatter away about anything that comes into their head from Paul Eluard to Raymond Chandler to Balzac, as their ride is diverted to a few stopovers. Meanwhile a budding romance blooms, as they eventually reach an inundated Paris.

REVIEWED ON 7/21/2013       GRADE: B
