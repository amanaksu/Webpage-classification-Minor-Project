MARGARET (director/writer: Kenneth Lonergan; cinematographer: Ryszard Lenczewski; editors: Anne McCabe/Michael Fay; music:  Nico Muhly; cast: Anna Paquin (Lisa Cohen), J. Smith-Cameron (Joan Cohen), Jean Reno (Ramon), Jeannie Berlin (Emily), Allison Janney (Monica Patterson), Matthew Broderick (John), Kieran Culkin (Paul), Mark Ruffalo (Maretti), Matt Damon (Mr. Aaron), Kenneth Lonergan (Lisa's screenwriter father), Cyrus Hernstadt (Curtis), John Gallagher Jr. (Darren); Runtime: 150; MPAA Rating: R; producers: Scott Rudin/Sydney Pollock/Gary Gilbert; Fox Searchlight; 2011)

 
"The coming-of-age pic has a sense of deeply felt gravity."

Reviewed by Dennis Schwartz

The title refers to a Gerard Manley Hopkins poem about grieving. The poem pops up during an English class lesson attended by the pic's student heroine, as Hopkins' emotionally charged Victorian poem, “Spring and Fall,” concerning a girl named Margaret, is read aloud.  Playwright director Kenneth Lonergan' ("You Can Count on Me") modern-day drama suffers from six years of legal woes involving production problems that were holding up its release, being overlong, too many unnecessary subplots to deal with (like the suffering guilt-stricken heroine's mom dating a smoothie opera loving SA computer businessman named Ramon), its wrenching heartfelt scenes strike a false note and its unbelievable dramatic contrivances become turn offs. But despite these flaws, the coming-of-age pic has a sense of deeply felt gravity that lingers in a shockingly haunting way as it questions idealism, morality, growing up and doing the right thing. It doesn't bring closure to the grieving process, but asks a lot of the hard questions that have to be asked about getting involved in tragedies in the despairing NYC  post-9/11 world.

The pic centers on the confused bright rich girl of entitlement, the 17-year-old New York City high-school student Lisa Cohen (Anna Paquin), a child of divorce, whose screenwriter father (Kenneth Lonergan) lives in LA. Lisa is at times bitchy, too opinionated and sarcastic, and not adverse to cheating on exams. She lives with her sister (Cyrus Hernstadt) and actress mom Joan (J. Smith-Cameron, the director's wife) in a luxury Upper West Side apartment. The film's main incident involves a cowboy wearing Manhattan bus driver, Maretti (Mark Ruffalo), distracted by Lisa running alongside the bus and flashing hand signals when he closes the door in her face and the driver while waving his hat at her jumps a red light and accidentally runs over a middle-aged woman pedestrian (Allison Janney). The graphic dying scene has Lisa trying to comfort the dying woman, who gets blood all over her and confides in her with her last breathes as if she were her daughter.

After Lisa lies at the deposition, saying the pedestrian crossed against the light, she has a change of heart when she comes into contact with the victim's aggressive best friend (Jeannie Berlin, Elaine May’s daughter) and the morally mixed-up teen changes her statement in an effort to assuage her guilt-feelings about indirectly causing the tragedy and then lying about it. Her change of testimony leads to a civil law suit for over $100,000 that goes against the city and the driver--charging him with vehicular negligence. Her action affects many lives.

Matt Damon plays Lisa's good guy concerned math teacher, whom she goes to for advice and to confess her erratic behavior.

REVIEWED ON 7/7/2013       GRADE: B

Dennis Schwartz: "Ozus' World Movie Reviews"
