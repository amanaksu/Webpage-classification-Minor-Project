    Samsung's Record Profits Hide Weaknesses Every Company Needs To Confront

    24 comments, 14 called-out
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    On Friday Samsung Electronics gave the markets guidance of record profits to come this quarter – but the stock price was unmoved by the news. Not quite as scary as Apple, you might say, where last year record profits led to a falling stock price.

    But let’s concentrate on Samsung, as they attempt to emulate the Apple business model (the new Smart Watch, Galaxy Gear is configured to work with Samsung Galaxy products, part of a longer term trend to close the loop on customers in the smartphone market).

    Here’s the problem and here is what Apple has right. Many major enterprises are trying to transition from product to device companies. For example, Microsoft from its base in software products to hardware devices.

    But the device is a multi-polar object. It’s not just a phone or tablet that communicates. It embraces hardware, software, connection (all those communities that might gather around it) and services (like iTunes).

    That also means it embraces several different business cultures. The culture that gives you great products isn’t necessarily interested in or good at giving you service (Samsung is a good example). Companies that work with connectivity don’t necessarily think of service either (Twitter, Facebook, Google, sell your data without telling you much about it, they depend on your igorance). Service companies normally hate hardware products – which is why financial services companies have yet to realize their future lies in the device and they need to do something about it.

    One of the problems in switching and mixing corporate cultures is that one group will have a traditional role in profitability. How come Xiaomi is capable of selling its product at cost? Well, because it doesn’t have hardware engineers who are used to making the lion’s share of the profit for the company. But if your engineers are steeped in the pride of innovation and margin success, switching to service and connection revenues can be very divisive – ask Nokia.

    Here’s a table summarizing what I mean. These are example of cultural problems that take root when you move from hardware to alternative revenue streams. None of these is a problem for Apple – organically they have outgrown them all. Each one hurts Samsung.

    How so, you might ask? Samsung runs an intense almost paranoid internal competitive culture (the CEO describes it as perpetual crisis) with teams that produce different product lines competing against other Samsung teams in the market. That’s fine when you are running dozens of new products into the market each year. It has accelerated Samsung’s hardware innovation cycles and made them unbeatable at product launch frequency.

    Now you have to ask those teams to slow down, because what you really want is less dependency on margin from hardware and more recurring revenue from services and apps. You need to shift your corporate mindset away from the race to launch yet more products and downgrade the incentives that lead to getting more stuff out of the door.

    In its place you need to start building and rewarding teams that will provide some new forms of revenue – ones that are as yet ill-defined that lie at the end of rocky roads as you try to build brand affinity in services and software and apps and community.

    Many companies are on this road, the fast road to a fully-fledged service company. Most are suffering some kind of bipolar disorder, a bifurcation of their culture in fact.  Not many have the right formula. There are a few that clearly have it – and Apple is the leader. Companies like CISCO have it in chaotic form. Samsung certainly does not and the question is whether its focus on hardware innovation strategy is in fact a permanent barrier. Big question for the markets: Can Samsung change its culture?
