    Can Merck Really Revive Itself By Jettisoning Employees?

    comments, called-out
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

     

    Merck & Co. President and CEO Kenneth Frazier (Image credit: Getty Images via @daylife)

    Last Tuesday, Merck chief executive Kenneth Frazier sent a message to the company’s employees.

    “Our size and our operating model have created inefficiencies and redundancies in the way we do business,” he wrote. “We can no longer afford to try and be all things to all people. This is not the time to simply tweak our processes and structures, this is the time for focused choices and precise execution if we want to succeed for years to come.”

    Merck announced that morning that it would cut 8,500 jobs over the next two years as part of a plan to reduce annual spending by $2.5 billion. The company says the cost cuts will be split approximately evenly between selling, general and administrative expenses and research and development. However, it seems likely the layoffs will hit hardest in Merck’s marketing division, which has the lion’s share of the company’s 81,000 employees. Only 13,600 people work in Merck’s research labs, where much of the money tends to be spent on expensive clinical trials to test new drugs, not headcount.

    Frazier says it was a “sad day” but an “essential” move to free up cash flow that can be returned to investors, used for acquisitions and licensing, and, most importantly, injected quickly into R&D efforts that show promise. Those operational efficiencies, Frazier says, are “a first step for what I believe will be a transformational change for the company.” Every indication is that the hardest part of that transformation is still up ahead.

    Even before Tuesday, the company planned 7,500 layoffs, mostly in manufacturing. All told, one in five people working at Merck will lose their jobs over the next few years. Then Merck will have shrunk another 20% from its current size, and 39% since 2009, after it purchased Schering-Plough for $41 billion. Each company employed more than 50,000 people, meaning an entire drug company’s worth of scientists, researchers and staff will have been laid off in just five years.

    Cuts had to happen due to the pressures dogging Merck and the industry as a whole. Even without the layoffs, analysts were forecasting that Merck would find at least $1 billion in cost savings over the next two years. The patent on the allergy drug Singulair expired last year, a lucrative joint venture with AstraZeneca is about to end, and Merck has seen delays or disappointments in R&D projects in heart disease, osteoporosis, insomnia and anesthesia. And there is some precedent to the idea that a research turnaround can happen as a company slims. Bristol-Myers Squibb has shrunk from 43,000 employees a decade ago to 28,000 today while developing one of the best research records in Big Pharma.

    But making this plan work will require fundamental changes in how Merck does business. It has traditionally funded lots of research projects that have each moved at their own pace, triumphing when it created drugs others had not thought of. Now it will focus on speed, keeping cash free to agilely pour into experimental programs that show sudden promise, and also trying to learn to buy or license medicines from outside the company, something it has not done effectively up to this point.

    In March Frazier replaced Peter Kim, who had run Merck research laboratories since 2003, with Roger Perlmutter, who had left Merck in 2001 to run R&D at Amgen. The layoff press release seemed like a repudiation of much of Merck’s R&D strategy over the past decade. Kim had bet heavily on buying so-called platform-based companies that didn’t come with experimental drugs but instead were supposed to help invent new ones. These included Rosetta Inpharmatics, for genetic data, continued to the RNAi silencing firm Sirna Therapeutics, and the protein production firm Glycofi. Merck now says it will “reduce its focus on platform technologies.” And oh, the company may try to stop or sell off some drugs in late-stage trials, too.

    Perlmutter is also trying to let the discovery operation drive the company’s direction, not the other way around. In its press release, Merck said it would focus commercially on diabetes, acute hospital care, vaccines and oncology. These are simply the areas where it has near-term opportunities with Januvia, its best-selling diabetes drug, its experimental anesthesia medicine, new vaccines against the viruses that cause cervical cancer and shingles, and a new cancer drug. As Perlmutter told me earlier this year, “Great drugs build great franchises, but great franchises don’t necessarily build great drugs. If you are too prescriptive with your R&D, you can spend an awful lot of money and not be terribly productive because there may actually not be any new mechanisms that you can get to right now that will help you in a particular disease area.”

    The focus under Perlmutter will be on getting products to market and, in his words, “eschewing distraction.” For Merck’s most promising cancer therapy, known as MK-3475, the company is funding an early stage study of unprecedented size and creating a new business unit to streamline the development and marketing of the drug. Merck’s hepatitis C drugs have been highlighted by Perlmutter as another promising area. Last Thursday, Mark Schoenebaum, the pharmaceuticals analyst at International Strategy & Investment Group, noticed that Merck had increased the size of a study of two hepatitis C drugs from 48 patients to 450, again apparently demonstrating this “all-in” strategy.

    But Merck’s cancer drug is still trailing a very similar medicine from Bristol-Myers Squibb, and in hepatitis C it is seen as behind efforts from Gilead, Bristol, and AbbVie. Perlmutter’s experience at Amgen shows the difficulty of being second, and also the challenges that come with trying to buy drugs from other companies. In 2005 Amgen spent $2.2 billion to acquire full rights to a drug called Vectibix, a cancer medicine that worked similarly to the blockbuster Erbitux. At the time, forecasts showed Vectibix eventually generating annual sales of $2 billion. Last year, Erbitux had global sales of $1.7 billion, Vectibix just $359 million. Perlmutter had better luck with a bone-strengthening drug invented at Amgen, which became a blockbuster, and with an experimental cholesterol drug that’s generating excitement among doctors and investors.

    In order to judge how these apparently big shifts will affect Merck’s future, both investors and Merck employees need a lot more information. Exactly where will layoffs hit? What costs will be cut? What, in particular, will the research labs look like and can it be conceivably argued that they’ll be better even if there are fewer scientists working in R&D?

    Frazier, despite saying that this is the beginning of transformational change, also insisted on a conference call with analysts last week that Merck is not changing its strategy. He notes that Merck is not entering new businesses, like generic drugs, nor spinning off units like its veterinary or consumer units – although he says Merck is constantly considering whether those businesses work better inside Merck, or outside it.

    “Maybe this may strike you as a better way of saying it,” he told me. “The what of the strategy is exactly the same, the how of how we execute the strategy across the company, especially in our commercial and R&D organizations, is a little bit different. We are being much more precise about where we allocate resources going forward, and that to me is what the distinction is.”

    If Frazier and Perlmutter are going to bring Merck back to its former place as the most respected of companies, they’re going to need to make that “a little bit different” sound like the understatement of the year.
